What Is TED?

About Thyroid Eye Disease

Thyroid Eye Disease (TED), also known as Graves' ophthalmopathy or thyroid-associated orbitopathy, is an autoimmune condition in which the immune system mistakenly attacks the muscles and fatty tissue behind and around the eyes.

TED is most commonly associated with Graves' disease, a condition that causes the thyroid gland to overproduce hormones. However, TED can also occur in people with normal or even underactive thyroid function.

The condition occurs in two phases:

  • Active phase: Inflammation and swelling occur, lasting 6 months to 2 years. This is the critical window for medical treatment.
  • Inactive (stable) phase: Inflammation subsides but physical changes — such as bulging eyes — may remain.

TED affects women more than men, typically occurring between ages 30–50, and smoking significantly worsens the condition.

🔬 What Causes TED?

TED is triggered when the immune system produces antibodies (TSH receptor antibodies / TRAb) that attack tissues in the thyroid and orbit, causing inflammation and swelling.

⚠️ Risk Factors

  • Graves' disease diagnosis
  • Smoking (doubles the risk)
  • Radioactive iodine treatment
  • Female sex
  • Family history

🩺 Who Treats TED?

A multidisciplinary team is often required: an endocrinologist, ophthalmologist, and oculoplastic surgeon working together for best outcomes.

Recognizing TED

Symptoms of Thyroid Eye Disease

TED symptoms range from mild to severe. Early recognition is critical — the active phase is the optimal treatment window.

👁️

Bulging Eyes (Proptosis)

One of the most recognizable signs — eyes appear to protrude or bulge forward due to swelling of fat and muscles behind the eye.

Common
😭

Dry, Gritty Eyes

Reduced ability to close eyelids completely leads to dryness, irritation, and a persistent gritty or sandy feeling.

Very Common
🔴

Redness & Swelling

Inflammation of the conjunctiva (white of the eye), eyelids, and surrounding tissues causes visible redness and puffiness.

Common
🌀

Double Vision (Diplopia)

Swelling of the extraocular muscles disrupts eye alignment, causing double vision — especially when looking up or sideways.

Moderate
☀️

Light Sensitivity

Photophobia (sensitivity to light) is common due to corneal exposure and inflammation, making bright environments painful.

Common
🚨

Vision Loss

In severe cases, compression of the optic nerve (dysthyroid optic neuropathy) can threaten permanent vision loss — a medical emergency.

Seek Care Immediately
⬆️

Eyelid Retraction

Upper eyelids pull back, creating a wide-eyed, staring appearance. This is a hallmark sign of TED, even in mild cases.

Common
💧

Excessive Tearing

As a response to dryness and irritation, the eyes may paradoxically produce excess tears, causing frequent watering.

Mild

⚡ Important: If you experience sudden vision changes, pain with eye movement, or rapid worsening symptoms — seek emergency ophthalmologic care immediately.

Medical Advances

Treatments for Thyroid Eye Disease

Treatment depends on the severity, phase of disease, and individual patient factors. Significant advances have been made in targeted therapies.

🟢 Mild TED

Lubricating Eye Drops

Artificial tears and lubricating ointments relieve dryness and protect the cornea from exposure damage.

Selenium Supplementation

Studies show selenium (100 mcg twice daily for 6 months) can improve mild TED and reduce progression — recommended by European thyroid guidelines.

Smoking Cessation

Stopping smoking is one of the most impactful interventions — smokers have more severe TED and poorer treatment responses.

Prism Glasses

Prisms added to eyeglass lenses can help manage mild double vision without surgery.

🟡 Moderate-to-Severe TED

FDA Approved 2020

Teprotumumab (Tepezza)

The first FDA-approved drug specifically for TED. A monoclonal antibody that blocks IGF-1R, dramatically reducing proptosis and double vision in clinical trials. Given as 8 IV infusions over 24 weeks.

Clinical results: ~83% of patients saw proptosis reduction; ~68% had improved double vision.

IV Glucocorticoids

High-dose intravenous methylprednisolone reduces orbital inflammation. Most effective during the active phase. Typically administered weekly for 12 weeks.

Orbital Radiation

Low-dose external beam radiation to the orbit reduces inflammation in select patients, often used in combination with steroids.

🔴 Severe / Stable Phase TED

Orbital Decompression Surgery

Removes bone and/or fat from the eye socket to create more space, reducing proptosis and relieving optic nerve compression.

Strabismus Surgery

Adjusts the position of eye muscles to correct double vision after the active phase has resolved and the condition is stable.

Eyelid Surgery

Corrects eyelid retraction, improves eye closure, and addresses cosmetic concerns once TED is no longer active.

🔬 Emerging & Pipeline Treatments (2024–2025)

Linsitinib (oral IGF-1R inhibitor)

Oral alternative to Tepezza currently in Phase 3 clinical trials — promising results for patients seeking non-infusion options.

Batoclimab (anti-FcRn)

Reduces TRAb antibody levels. Phase 3 trials underway — may address the underlying autoimmune cause more directly.

Istabimab (anti-IGF-1R)

Another next-generation monoclonal antibody targeting the IGF-1R pathway with potentially fewer side effects.

Subcutaneous Teprotumumab

A subcutaneous (under-skin injection) formulation of Tepezza is in development, offering a more convenient self-administration option.
